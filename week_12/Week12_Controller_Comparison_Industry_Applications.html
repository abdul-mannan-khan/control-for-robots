Section 3: Part 1 — Comprehensive Controller Comparison

In this capstone lecture, we step back from individual controller design and take a holistic view of all seven control methods studied across Weeks 1–8. The goal is to understand not just how each controller works, but when and why to choose one over another. This is the skill that separates a textbook reader from a practising control engineer.

3.1: The Seven Controllers at a Glance

Each card below summarises a controller's core idea, key equation, strengths, weaknesses, and ideal use case.

1. Computed Torque Control (CTC) — Week 1

Feedback linearisation via exact model cancellation

\[\tau = M(q)\bigl(\ddot{q}_d + K_d \dot{e} + K_p e\bigr) + C(q,\dot{q})\dot{q} + G(q)\]

Strengths: Exact linearisation yields decoupled error dynamics; straightforward PD tuning after cancellation; excellent tracking when model is accurate.

Weaknesses: Requires highly accurate dynamic model; no inherent robustness to parameter uncertainty.

Ideal use: Well-calibrated industrial manipulators with known payloads (e.g., FANUC, KUKA in structured cells).

2. Model Predictive Control (MPC) — Week 3

Receding-horizon optimisation with constraint satisfaction

\[\min_{u_{0:N-1}} \sum_{k=0}^{N-1}\bigl( x_k^T Q\, x_k + u_k^T R\, u_k \bigr) + x_N^T P_f\, x_N \quad \text{s.t. } x \in \mathcal{X},\; u \in \mathcal{U}\]

Strengths: Systematic constraint handling; preview/anticipation of future reference; flexible multi-objective cost.

Weaknesses: High computational cost (online optimisation); performance degrades with model inaccuracy.

Ideal use: Autonomous vehicles, constrained navigation, any system where actuator/state limits are critical.

3. Adaptive Control — Week 4

Online parameter estimation with concurrent control

\[\tau = Y(q,\dot{q},\dot{q}_r,\ddot{q}_r)\,\hat{\theta}, \qquad \dot{\hat{\theta}} = -\Gamma Y^T s\]

Strengths: Handles unknown/slowly varying parameters; Lyapunov convergence guarantees; no prior system ID needed.

Weaknesses: Requires regressor form (linearity in parameters); poor transient; needs persistent excitation.

Ideal use: Manipulators with changing payloads, robots in unstructured environments.

4. Robust Control (H-infinity) — Week 5

Worst-case performance optimisation over uncertainty sets

\[\min_K \;\|T_{zw}(s)\|_\infty = \min_K \;\sup_\omega \bar{\sigma}\bigl(T_{zw}(j\omega)\bigr) < \gamma\]

Strengths: Guaranteed performance despite bounded uncertainty; systematic LMI/Riccati framework; excellent for structured perturbations.

Weaknesses: Conservative (worst-case optimisation); complex design requiring frequency-domain expertise.

Ideal use: Safety-critical systems (aerospace, surgical robots) requiring guaranteed stability margins.

5. Backstepping Control — Week 6

Recursive Lyapunov-based design for strict-feedback systems

\[V_i = V_{i-1} + \tfrac{1}{2}z_i^2, \qquad z_i = x_i - \alpha_{i-1}(x_1,\ldots,x_{i-1})\]

Strengths: Constructive Lyapunov guarantees global asymptotic stability; natural for cascaded structures; smooth control law.

Weaknesses: Restricted to strict-feedback form; "explosion of terms" in higher-order systems.

Ideal use: Underactuated systems (quadrotors, marine vessels), VTOL transition control.

6. Sliding Mode Control (SMC) — Week 7

Variable-structure control with discontinuous switching

\[\tau = \tau_{eq} - K\,\text{sign}(s), \qquad s = \dot{e} + \Lambda e\]

Strengths: Invariant to matched disturbances; finite-time convergence to sliding surface; simple structure.

Weaknesses: Chattering from discontinuous control; only handles matched uncertainties.

Ideal use: Systems with significant matched disturbances: outdoor drones in wind, CNC machining.

7. Nonlinear Control Optimization — Week 8

Pontryagin / iLQR / NMPC on the nonlinear plant; also optimization of the gains of Controllers 1–6

\[\min_{u(\cdot)}\ J = \Phi(x(T)) + \int_0^T L(x,u,t)\,dt \quad\text{s.t. } \dot x = f(x,u)\]

Strengths: Operates on the nonlinear model directly (no linearisation); iLQR gives a local feedback policy in addition to the open-loop plan; provides a unified framework to optimize the parameters of every other controller (SMC \(\lambda,\phi,\eta\); adaptive \(\Gamma,\sigma\); robust \(K_p,K_d,\rho,\Lambda\); backstepping \(k_1,\ldots,k_n\)).

Weaknesses: Iterative; local (not global) optimum; needs derivatives (finite difference or autodiff); real-time requires warm-starting and fast SQP / iLQR back-ends.

Ideal use: Trajectory optimization for manipulators / drones (iLQR / DDP), receding-horizon tracking (NMPC), and offline tuning of any controller from 1–6 via min-max or Bayesian search.

3.6: When to Use Which Controller — Practitioner's Decision Guide

Practical Decision Guide

Start with the simplest method that meets your requirements. A PID or LQR that a technician can tune is often preferable to an MPC that requires a PhD to debug.

  • Accurate model + trajectory tracking: Begin with Computed Torque Control. Use it as your benchmark.
  • Hard constraints must never be violated: MPC is not a luxury — it is a necessity.
  • Unknown or time-varying parameters: Adaptive Control is designed for exactly this scenario.
  • Significant matched disturbances: Sliding Mode Control provides mathematically guaranteed rejection.
  • Formal robustness certificates for safety-critical systems: Robust H-infinity provides the strongest guarantees.
  • Cascaded strict-feedback nonlinear dynamics: Backstepping gives constructive Lyapunov stability at each step.
  • Aggressive motion requiring prediction over a nonlinear horizon (trajectory-level): Nonlinear Optimization (iLQR/NMPC) — operates directly on the nonlinear model, provides a time-varying feedback policy alongside the open-loop plan.
  • In practice, the best controllers are hybrids. DJI uses cascaded PID with LQR. Boston Dynamics uses CTC + MPC. Tesla uses MPC + LQR. The methods are components of an architecture.

Section 4: Part 2 — Industry Applications & Real-World Case Studies

4.1: Automotive & Self-Driving Vehicles

Tesla Autopilot / FSD

MPC for trajectory planning: convex optimisation generates smooth lane-change trajectories respecting speed, acceleration, and lane-boundary constraints. LQR for lateral control: linearised bicycle model with optimal steering gains. Neural network perception feeds into a classical MPC-LQR control stack.

Waymo (Google)

MPC for trajectory planning with safety constraints encoded as hard inequalities. LQR for longitudinal/lateral low-level tracking. Sensor fusion via EKF (the observer in LQG). Published research highlights convex MPC solving at 10 Hz on custom compute.

BMW / Mercedes Active Suspension

H-infinity robust control for active suspension: road surface is an unmeasured disturbance with bounded energy. The controller minimises the \(\mathcal{H}_\infty\) norm of the transfer function from road input to passenger acceleration, guaranteeing ride comfort over all road profiles within the uncertainty set.

Toyota Adaptive Cruise Control

MPC with safety constraints: minimum following distance (2-second rule), speed limits, and comfort bounds on acceleration/jerk. The prediction horizon anticipates traffic flow changes, enabling smoother braking than reactive PID.

Formula 1 Energy Recovery (ERS/KERS)

MPC for energy management: optimises the deployment and harvesting of electrical energy over a lap, subject to battery constraints and FIA regulations. Traction control uses SMC for rapid wheel-slip regulation under extreme torque.

Tesla / Rivian AWD Torque Vectoring

LQR for torque distribution across 4 independent motors: minimises a cost function balancing yaw tracking error against total energy consumption. The linearised vehicle model around the current operating point makes LQR ideal for this real-time application.

Bosch Electronic Stability Program (ESP)

Sliding Mode Control for electronic stability: the SMC switching function drives the vehicle's yaw rate to the desired value despite unknown road friction (matched disturbance). Super-twisting SMC variant avoids brake chattering.

Autonomous Trucks (TuSimple, Aurora)

MPC for lane keeping with longer prediction horizons (trucks have larger inertia and stopping distances). Constraints include lane boundaries, speed limits, and vehicle dynamics limits. Adaptive elements compensate for varying cargo mass.

Electric Power Steering (EPS)

LQR-based torque assist: the controller minimises a cost that balances steering effort (driver comfort) against road-feel feedback. Gain-scheduled across vehicle speed for optimal performance at parking vs highway speeds.

Autonomous Parking (Bosch, Valeo)

MPC with nonholonomic constraints: the car's kinematic model (Ackermann steering) has turning radius limits that MPC naturally encodes. The finite horizon plans the entire parking manoeuvre as a constrained trajectory optimisation.

4.2: Aerospace & UAVs

DJI Consumer Drones (Mavic, Mini)

Cascaded PID — the industry standard: inner attitude-rate loop at 500 Hz, attitude loop at 250 Hz, position loop at 50 Hz. Simple, robust, and tunable by firmware engineers. PID accounts for >90% of commercial drone controllers.

Skydio Autonomous Drones

MPC for real-time obstacle avoidance and aggressive manoeuvres. The onboard NVIDIA Jetson solves convex MPC at 50 Hz with obstacle constraints from visual SLAM. Published at RSS/ICRA showing MPC-based trajectory planning through forests.

Boeing 787 Autopilot

LQR-based autopilot for cruise flight with gain scheduling across flight envelope (altitude, speed, weight). Robust control for flutter suppression: \(\mathcal{H}_\infty\) methods ensure aeroelastic stability across all structural modes.

Airbus A380 Fly-by-Wire

H-infinity robust control for flight control laws: the A380 uses \(\mu\)-synthesis (structured singular value) to guarantee stability over the full flight envelope despite parametric uncertainty in aerodynamic coefficients. Certified by EASA to DO-178C standards.

NASA Ingenuity Mars Helicopter

LQR + Kalman filter (LQG) for flight in Mars' thin atmosphere (1% of Earth's density). The Kalman filter fuses IMU data with a laser altimeter and downward-facing camera for state estimation. LQR provides optimal hover control with minimal computational footprint on the Snapdragon 801 processor.

SpaceX Falcon 9 Landing

Convex MPC (powered descent guidance): fuel-optimal trajectory computed via successive convexification. The controller plans the landing trajectory in real time, subject to thrust magnitude/direction constraints, glide-slope constraints, and the "landing on a moving barge" constraint set. Published in AIAA/AAS conferences.

Zipline Medical Delivery Drones

Adaptive control for varying payload mass: blood products and vaccines have different weights (0.5–2.5 kg), and the controller adapts online during each delivery flight. Fixed-wing transition uses backstepping for the cascaded aerodynamic model.

Military UAVs (MQ-9 Reaper)

SMC for robustness to wind gusts and battle damage (partial wing loss changes aerodynamic model dramatically). The invariance property ensures the aircraft remains controllable as long as remaining control surfaces provide sufficient authority.

Wing (Alphabet) Delivery

Backstepping for VTOL-to-cruise transition: the cascaded dynamics (hover attitude → wing-borne flight) are naturally in strict-feedback form. Backstepping provides smooth transition control with global stability guarantees.

Joby Aviation eVTOL

MPC for urban air mobility: constrained trajectory optimisation considering no-fly zones, noise abatement corridors, and battery energy management. The eVTOL's tilt-rotor transition is managed by gain-scheduled LQR.

4.3: Industrial Robotics & Manufacturing

FANUC (Pick-and-Place)

Computed Torque Control: FANUC's proprietary servo system uses inverse dynamics cancellation with PD error feedback. Cycle times under 0.5 seconds with sub-millimetre repeatability. The well-known, fixed payload makes CTC ideal.

ABB IRB (Palletising)

Adaptive control for variable-payload palletising: boxes from 0.5 kg to 30 kg require different inertial compensation. ABB's TrueMove technology uses feedforward model adaptation for consistent path accuracy regardless of payload.

KUKA iiwa (Human Collaboration)

Impedance / Computed Torque for safe human-robot interaction: joint torque sensors enable compliant behaviour (virtual spring-damper). CTC provides the nominal trajectory tracking while impedance control modulates stiffness on contact.

Yaskawa Motoman (Arc Welding)

SMC for disturbance rejection during arc welding: cutting forces, arc blow, and spatter create matched disturbances that SMC's switching term rejects. Super-twisting variant keeps the weld bead smooth.

Universal Robots UR5/UR10

Adaptive control for flexible manufacturing: UR cobots are deployed in SMEs where payloads change frequently. The controller adapts to new end-effectors without manual re-calibration, reducing setup time from hours to minutes.

Siemens SINUMERIK CNC

Adaptive control for tool wear compensation: as cutting tools wear, cutting forces increase. The adaptive controller adjusts feed rate and spindle speed to maintain surface quality, extending tool life by 15–30%.

Engel Injection Moulding

MPC for temperature and pressure profile optimisation: the multi-zone heating system requires coordinated control with constraints on maximum temperature gradients and cycle time. MPC's preview capability optimises the entire injection cycle.

Semiconductor Wafer Handling

LQR for vibration-free motion: wafer transport requires sub-micrometre positioning accuracy with zero residual vibration. LQR minimises a cost penalising both position error and jerk (vibration-inducing acceleration changes).

Laser Cutting Systems

SMC for contour following: the cutting head must follow complex 2D/3D contours despite varying material thickness and thermal distortion. SMC's invariance property ensures cutting accuracy within ±0.05 mm.

3D Printing / Additive Manufacturing

Robust control for layer consistency: extrusion rate, nozzle temperature, and build-plate adhesion vary layer to layer. Robust controllers maintain dimensional accuracy despite these slowly varying parametric uncertainties.

4.4: Warehouse & Logistics Robotics

Amazon Robotics (Kiva / Proteus)

MPC for fleet coordination: each robot solves a local MPC that respects velocity constraints, keepout zones, and priority rules for intersection crossing. Low-level drive control uses PID for wheel velocity regulation.

Locus Robotics

MPC-based local planner for warehouse navigation: replaces DWA with a constrained MPC that handles narrow aisles and dynamic obstacles (human workers). Published benchmarks show 30% fewer near-misses vs DWA.

Fetch Robotics

LQR for the mobile base (smooth, energy-efficient motion) combined with PID for the manipulator arm. The separation of control strategies reflects the different dynamics (wheeled base vs articulated arm).

Ocado (Grocery Fulfilment)

MPC for multi-robot deconfliction on the grid: hundreds of robots navigate a grid simultaneously. MPC plans collision-free paths with time-slot constraints at grid intersections. Centralised planning with decentralised execution.

Magazino TORU

Adaptive grasping for items of varying mass and shape: the gripper controller adapts grip force online based on tactile sensor feedback, preventing damage to fragile items while securely holding heavy ones.

AGVs (Dematic, KION)

PID + feedforward for line-following: magnetic tape or painted line guidance with PID lateral correction. Feedforward from the known path curvature anticipates steering adjustments. Simple, reliable, maintainable by warehouse technicians.

Autonomous Forklifts (Seegrid)

Robust control for heavy-load handling: when carrying 2000+ kg pallets, the vehicle dynamics change dramatically. Robust H-infinity methods ensure stability across the full load range without re-tuning.

4.5: Legged Robotics

Boston Dynamics Spot

Convex MPC for whole-body locomotion: the single rigid body model is linearised around the current state, and a QP is solved at 30 Hz to compute ground reaction forces for all four feet. Published by MIT in "Mini Cheetah" and adapted by BD.

Boston Dynamics Atlas

MPC + whole-body control for parkour and dynamic manoeuvres: hierarchical architecture with MPC at the planning level (contact schedule, CoM trajectory) and operational-space CTC at the joint level (tracking desired contact forces and body motion).

ANYbotics ANYmal

MPC with zero-moment-point (ZMP) constraints for industrial inspection on uneven terrain. Combines convex MPC for gait generation with robust control for disturbance rejection on slippery surfaces.

Agility Robotics Digit

LQR for walking stabilisation (linearised around the nominal gait) combined with MPC for step planning and footstep optimisation. The bipedal structure requires careful management of the zero-moment point.

MIT Mini Cheetah

Convex MPC for trot, bound, and pronk gaits: the seminal paper (Di Carlo et al., 2018) demonstrated that a single convex MPC formulation can generate multiple gaits simply by changing the contact schedule. Solved in <3 ms on an Intel i7.

Unitree Go2

Cascaded PID + MPC for consumer quadruped locomotion: reinforcement learning generates the gait policy, which is tracked by a low-level PID/MPC controller. Combines learning-based planning with model-based control for reliability.

Passive Dynamic Walkers

LQR for energy-efficient gait stabilisation: the natural dynamics of the walker are nearly periodic, and LQR about the limit cycle provides minimal-energy perturbation correction. Demonstrates that optimal control can exploit natural dynamics.

4.6: Medical & Surgical Robotics

Intuitive Surgical da Vinci

Robust control for teleoperation with communication delay: the master-slave architecture must remain stable despite variable network latency (0.1–0.5 s). Passivity-based robust control ensures coupled stability. Force scaling uses impedance control for delicate tissue manipulation.

Stryker Mako (Orthopaedic)

Adaptive control for bone cutting: bone density and hardness vary between patients and anatomical locations. The controller adapts cutting force and feed rate online based on force/torque sensor feedback, preventing thermal necrosis.

Medtronic Hugo RAS

Force-limited computed torque: CTC provides precise trajectory tracking for the instrument tip, with a force limiter that reduces torque commands when contact forces exceed safety thresholds. Certified for surgical use under IEC 62304.

Rehabilitation Exoskeletons (Ekso, ReWalk)

Adaptive impedance control: the exoskeleton adapts its assistance level based on the patient's effort (measured via EMG or interaction force). As the patient recovers, the controller reduces assistance — "assist-as-needed" paradigm uses adaptive gain scheduling.

CyberKnife (Accuray)

LQR for positioning the linear accelerator + SMC for tumour tracking during respiratory motion. The tumour moves ~15 mm with breathing; SMC's finite-time convergence ensures the radiation beam tracks within 0.5 mm despite the quasi-periodic disturbance.

Surgical Needle Insertion

Backstepping for nonlinear tissue interaction: needle insertion through layered tissue (skin, fat, muscle) involves highly nonlinear force-displacement characteristics. Backstepping handles the cascaded structure (needle position → tip force → tissue deformation).

4.7: Marine & Underwater Robotics

Saab Seaeye ROVs

SMC for deep-sea current rejection: ocean currents are large, persistent, matched disturbances. SMC provides guaranteed station-keeping despite currents up to 3 knots. The "sliding" property is particularly valuable when current measurements are unavailable.

Kongsberg AUVs

LQR for heading and depth control during survey missions: the linearised hydrodynamic model supports optimal LQR design. The quadratic cost naturally balances heading accuracy against thruster energy, extending mission duration.

Rolls-Royce Dynamic Positioning

MPC for station-keeping of drilling vessels: the vessel must maintain position within ±2 m despite wind, waves, and current. MPC coordinates 6+ thrusters with constraints on thrust rate, power allocation, and forbidden thrust sectors (near the drilling riser).

Sea Machines Autonomous Surface Vessels

Robust control for wave disturbance rejection: sea states create broadband disturbances that cannot be modelled exactly. H-infinity methods provide worst-case performance guarantees across sea states 1–6.

Torpedo Guidance Systems

SMC for terminal guidance: ocean currents and target manoeuvres are treated as matched disturbances. The sliding mode ensures the guidance error reaches zero in finite time, critical for interception accuracy.

4.8: Space & Satellite Systems

Hubble Space Telescope

LQR for 0.007 arcsecond pointing accuracy: reaction wheels provide fine attitude control, with LQR optimising the trade-off between pointing error and wheel momentum accumulation. The well-modelled, linear dynamics make LQR the ideal choice.

ISS Canadarm2

Computed Torque Control in microgravity: the absence of gravity simplifies the dynamics (\(G(q)=0\)), making CTC highly effective. The known payload (ISS modules, astronauts) allows accurate model-based control for delicate berthing operations.

James Webb Space Telescope

LQR fine guidance: the Fine Guidance Sensor (FGS) provides star positions to the attitude controller, which uses LQR to achieve milli-arcsecond pointing stability. The extremely low disturbance environment (L2 Lagrange point) makes LQR optimal.

Satellite Attitude Control (Reaction Wheels)

NMPC / iLQR is the standard for planning complex pointing manoeuvres. SMC for fault-tolerant mode: when a reaction wheel fails, the control authority changes and SMC's robustness to matched uncertainty provides graceful degradation.

SpaceX Starship Re-entry

MPC for re-entry guidance: the "belly-flop" manoeuvre requires trajectory optimisation with aerodynamic heating constraints, structural load limits, and landing accuracy. Adaptive elements handle the aerodynamic uncertainty during hypersonic flight.

Mars Rovers (Curiosity, Perseverance)

Robust control for unknown terrain: wheel-terrain interaction models are highly uncertain on Mars. Adaptive control compensates for wheel degradation over the multi-year mission (Curiosity's wheels show significant wear after 10+ years).

4.9: Energy & Process Control

Oil Refineries (Honeywell, Emerson)

MPC dominates: over 90% of advanced process control installations in oil/gas use MPC (Dynamic Matrix Control, RMPCT). Multi-variable, constrained optimisation of temperature, pressure, and flow across 10–100 process variables simultaneously.

Wind Turbine Pitch Control

Adaptive + Robust: wind speed and direction change continuously (unknown parameters), while turbulent gusts create disturbances. Adaptive control adjusts the pitch gain schedule, while robust inner loops maintain structural integrity under extreme gusts.

Nuclear Reactor Control

Robust control with large safety margins: nuclear safety regulations require proven stability margins under all credible uncertainty scenarios. H-infinity methods provide the formal certificates required by nuclear regulators (NRC, ONR).

Smart Grid Frequency Regulation

MPC for demand-response: coordinates battery storage, flexible loads, and generators to maintain grid frequency at 50/60 Hz. The prediction horizon captures the slow dynamics of thermal generators and the fast response of batteries.

Google DeepMind Data Centre HVAC

MPC (model-based reinforcement learning): reduced cooling energy by 40% by optimising chiller setpoints, fan speeds, and damper positions subject to thermal constraints. The "model" is learned from historical data, making this a data-driven MPC.

Solar Panel Dual-Axis Tracking

LQR for smooth, energy-efficient sun tracking: the cost function penalises both tracking error (reduced solar yield) and motor energy (parasitic losses). The gentle, predictable solar motion makes linearised LQR sufficient.

4.10: Consumer & Emerging Applications

Segway / Ninebot

LQR for inverted pendulum stabilisation: the classic application of LQR. The linearised model around the upright equilibrium is accurate for small angles, and LQR provides optimal state feedback for balancing with minimum rider discomfort.

iRobot Roomba

DJI Agras (Agricultural Drones)

DJI Ronin Camera Gimbals

Autonomous Lawnmowers (Husqvarna)

Final Exam Preparation Quiz — 5 Questions

Q1: Which controller is the ONLY one that natively enforces hard state and input constraints?

Answer: MPC — constraints are encoded as inequalities in the optimisation problem.

Q2: A quadrotor must hover in 20 mph wind with minimal computation. Which controller and why?

Answer: SMC — wind is a matched disturbance; SMC's invariance property provides guaranteed rejection with O(n) computation (no online optimisation). Use super-twisting to avoid chattering.

Q3: Why does LQG lose the guaranteed robustness margins of LQR?

Answer: Doyle (1978) showed that the Kalman filter can arbitrarily degrade the phase/gain margins guaranteed by LQR. The separation principle ensures optimal performance for the nominal stochastic system but provides no robustness guarantees when the model is uncertain. This motivates LQG/LTR (Loop Transfer Recovery).

Q4: Name two industry applications where Adaptive Control is the primary method and explain why.

Answer: (1) Universal Robots UR5 in flexible manufacturing — payloads change frequently and the controller must adapt without re-calibration. (2) Zipline delivery drones — payload mass varies per delivery (blood, vaccines, 0.5–2.5 kg), requiring online adaptation of the flight controller.

Q5: In the ROS 2 controller node architecture, what are the standard subscriber and publisher topics for a mobile robot controller?

Answer: Subscribe to /odom (nav_msgs/Odometry — state feedback) and /reference_trajectory (desired path). Publish to /cmd_vel (geometry_msgs/Twist — control output) and /controller_diagnostics (error, computation time).

Appendix: Review Material — Navigation Control

Question 1 — DWA vs MPC for Mobile Robot Navigation (25 marks)

Compare the Dynamic Window Approach (DWA) and Model Predictive Control (MPC) as local controllers for mobile robot navigation.

(a) In what sense is DWA a special case of MPC? Explain the structural similarities and differences. (10 marks)

(b) When would you prefer Model Predictive Path Integral (MPPI) control over DWA? Give three specific scenarios with justification. (10 marks)

(c) Describe how hard constraints (obstacle avoidance, velocity limits) are handled in each approach. (5 marks)

Model Answer

(a) DWA as a special case of MPC:

DWA and MPC share the same fundamental structure: at each time step, both (1) predict future states over a finite horizon, (2) evaluate candidate trajectories against a cost function, and (3) select the best action. The key differences are:

  • Action space: DWA samples a discrete grid of \((v, \omega)\) pairs within the dynamic window (admissible velocities reachable in one time step), whereas MPC optimises over a continuous control sequence \(u_{0:N-1}\) using gradient-based or QP solvers.
  • Prediction model: DWA uses a simplified kinematic model (constant-velocity arcs), while MPC can use the full nonlinear dynamic model.
  • Horizon: DWA typically uses a short prediction horizon (1–3 seconds with fixed velocity), while MPC explicitly optimises over N discrete steps with time-varying controls.
  • Optimality: DWA finds the best among sampled candidates (combinatorial), while MPC finds a local optimum of the continuous cost (gradient-based). DWA is MPC with a sampled action space and single-step constant-velocity prediction.

(b) When to prefer MPPI over DWA:

  • Scenario 1 — Highly cluttered environments: MPPI samples thousands of trajectories (not just constant-velocity arcs) using GPU-parallel rollouts, covering a much richer space of manoeuvres. In tight spaces with narrow passages, MPPI finds feasible paths that DWA's limited arc-based sampling misses.
  • Scenario 2 — Nonlinear cost functions: MPPI handles arbitrary (non-convex, non-differentiable) cost functions since it uses sampling rather than gradient-based optimisation. When the cost includes discrete penalties (e.g., semantic zone costs, social navigation rules), MPPI naturally handles them while DWA's scoring function is more restrictive.
  • Scenario 3 — Dynamic obstacles: MPPI's longer and more diverse prediction horizons capture interactions with moving obstacles (pedestrians, other robots). The stochastic sampling naturally explores evasive manoeuvres that DWA's deterministic arc evaluation cannot anticipate.

(c) Constraint handling:

  • DWA: Velocity constraints are enforced by the dynamic window (only admissible velocities are sampled). Obstacle constraints are handled by scoring: trajectories that intersect obstacles receive a very high (penalised) cost or are discarded. This is soft constraint handling — there is no formal guarantee of feasibility.
  • MPC: Constraints are encoded as explicit inequality constraints in the optimisation: \(x \in \mathcal{X}\), \(u \in \mathcal{U}\), \(h(x) \geq d_{\text{safe}}\). The solver guarantees that the returned solution satisfies all constraints (if feasible). This is hard constraint handling with formal guarantees.

Question 2 — AMCL and State Estimation for Navigation Control (25 marks)

Explain how Adaptive Monte Carlo Localisation (AMCL) provides state estimation for the navigation control loop. Draw parallels to the Kalman filter used in LQG (Week 8).

(a) Describe the AMCL algorithm (particle filter) and explain its role in providing state estimates \([\hat{x}, \hat{y}, \hat{\theta}]\) to the controller. (10 marks)

(b) Compare AMCL (particle filter) with the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) used in LQG. Under what conditions would you prefer each? (10 marks)

(c) Explain how uncertainty in the AMCL estimate propagates to the controller and affects closed-loop performance. (5 marks)

Model Answer

(a) AMCL algorithm:

AMCL is a particle filter implementation for robot localisation given a known map. It maintains a set of \(N\) weighted particles \(\{(x_i, y_i, \theta_i, w_i)\}_{i=1}^N\), each representing a hypothesis of the robot's pose.

  1. Prediction: When the robot moves (odometry \(\Delta x, \Delta y, \Delta\theta\)), each particle is propagated using the motion model with added noise: \(x_i' = f(x_i, u) + \epsilon\). This is analogous to the Kalman filter prediction step \(\hat{x}^- = A\hat{x} + Bu\).
  2. Update: When a LiDAR scan arrives, each particle's weight is updated based on how well the scan matches the map from that particle's pose: \(w_i = p(z | x_i, \text{map})\). This is analogous to the Kalman update with innovation \(y - C\hat{x}\).
  3. Resampling: Particles with low weights are replaced by copies of high-weight particles (with noise). This concentrates particles around likely poses. The "adaptive" aspect adjusts \(N\) based on the spread of particle weights — fewer particles when confident, more when uncertain.
  4. Output: The state estimate \([\hat{x}, \hat{y}, \hat{\theta}]\) is the weighted mean of all particles: \(\hat{x} = \sum_i w_i x_i\). This estimate is published on /amcl_pose and consumed by the navigation controller.

(b) AMCL vs EKF comparison:

FeatureAMCL (Particle Filter)EKF (Kalman Filter)
DistributionArbitrary (multimodal)Gaussian (unimodal)
Global localisationYes (particles spread globally)No (requires initial estimate)
Computational costO(N) per step, N = 500–5000O(n^3) per step, n = state dim
Nonlinearity handlingExact (samples from true distribution)Approximate (first-order Taylor)
Curse of dimensionalityScales poorly beyond ~6DScales well to high dimensions

Prefer AMCL when: (1) the initial pose is unknown (global localisation / kidnapped robot), (2) the environment has symmetries causing multimodal distributions, (3) the state space is low-dimensional (2D + heading). Prefer EKF when: (1) the state is high-dimensional (6-DOF drone), (2) good initial estimates are available, (3) real-time performance is critical on embedded hardware.

(c) Uncertainty propagation to the controller:

The AMCL estimate has covariance \(\Sigma_{\text{pose}}\) derived from the particle spread. When this uncertain estimate is fed to the controller (e.g., MPC tracking a path), the controller acts on \(\hat{x}\) rather than the true \(x\). The tracking error becomes \(e = x_{\text{ref}} - \hat{x} + (\hat{x} - x)\), where \(\hat{x} - x\) is the estimation error. High AMCL uncertainty (e.g., in featureless corridors) directly degrades tracking performance. This motivates the LQG separation principle: design the controller assuming perfect state knowledge, then account for estimation noise through the observer (Kalman filter or particle filter). However, unlike LQG, the separation principle does not hold optimally for nonlinear systems with particle filters, so the combined estimator-controller performance is suboptimal.
