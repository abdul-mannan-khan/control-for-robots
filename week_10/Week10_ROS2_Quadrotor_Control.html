Section 3: Part 1 — Quadrotor Dynamics Review

You studied quadrotor dynamics extensively in Weeks 3–8 (MATLAB theory block). Here we briefly review the key equations that we will now implement in ROS2 control nodes. The simulation uses the quadrotor_sim.py physics engine from src/ros2_robotics_course/week_06/, which follows the ENU/FLU convention (x-forward, y-left, z-up) — the ROS2 standard.

3.1 State Vector

The full quadrotor state is a 12-dimensional vector:

\[ \mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x & y & z & \phi & \theta & \psi & \dot{x} & \dot{y} & \dot{z} & p & q & r \end{bmatrix}^T \]

where \((x, y, z)\) is the position in the world ENU frame, \((\phi, \theta, \psi)\) are roll, pitch, yaw (ZYX Euler angles), \((\dot{x}, \dot{y}, \dot{z})\) are world-frame velocities, and \((p, q, r)\) are body-frame angular rates.

3.2 Translational Dynamics

Applying Newton's second law in the world frame:

\[ m\ddot{\mathbf{p}} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ -mg \end{bmatrix} + R(\phi,\theta,\psi) \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ T \end{bmatrix} \]

where \(m\) is the total mass, \(g = 9.81\;\text{m/s}^2\), \(T\) is the total thrust along the body z-axis, and \(R(\phi,\theta,\psi)\) is the ZYX rotation matrix (body-to-world):

\[ R = R_z(\psi) R_y(\theta) R_x(\phi) = \begin{bmatrix} c\theta c\psi & c\psi s\theta s\phi - s\psi c\phi & c\psi s\theta c\phi + s\psi s\phi \\ c\theta s\psi & s\psi s\theta s\phi + c\psi c\phi & s\psi s\theta c\phi - c\psi s\phi \\ -s\theta & c\theta s\phi & c\theta c\phi \end{bmatrix} \]

3.3 Rotational Dynamics

Euler's equation for rotational motion in the body frame:

\[ J\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}} = -\boldsymbol{\omega} \times J\boldsymbol{\omega} + \boldsymbol{\tau} \]

where \(J = \text{diag}(I_{xx}, I_{yy}, I_{zz})\) is the inertia tensor (diagonal for a symmetric quadrotor), \(\boldsymbol{\omega} = [p, q, r]^T\) are body angular rates, and \(\boldsymbol{\tau} = [\tau_\phi, \tau_\theta, \tau_\psi]^T\) are the control torques.

Expanding component-wise:

\[ \begin{aligned} I_{xx}\dot{p} &= (I_{yy} - I_{zz})qr + \tau_\phi \\ I_{yy}\dot{q} &= (I_{zz} - I_{xx})pr + \tau_\theta \\ I_{zz}\dot{r} &= (I_{xx} - I_{yy})pq + \tau_\psi \end{aligned} \]

3.4 Motor Mixing Matrix

The four motors (plus-configuration) produce a total thrust \(T\) and three torques \((\tau_\phi, \tau_\theta, \tau_\psi)\). The relationship is:

\[ \begin{bmatrix} T \\ \tau_\phi \\ \tau_\theta \\ \tau_\psi \end{bmatrix} = \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} k_f & k_f & k_f & k_f \\ 0 & -dk_f & 0 & dk_f \\ dk_f & 0 & -dk_f & 0 \\ -k_m & k_m & -k_m & k_m \end{bmatrix}}_{\displaystyle M} \begin{bmatrix} \omega_1^2 \\ \omega_2^2 \\ \omega_3^2 \\ \omega_4^2 \end{bmatrix} \]

where \(k_f\) is the thrust coefficient, \(k_m\) is the torque coefficient, \(d\) is the arm length, and \(\omega_i\) is the angular speed of motor \(i\). The inverse mixing matrix \(M^{-1}\) converts desired thrust/torques to motor speed commands:

\[ \begin{bmatrix} \omega_1^2 \\ \omega_2^2 \\ \omega_3^2 \\ \omega_4^2 \end{bmatrix} = M^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} T \\ \tau_\phi \\ \tau_\theta \\ \tau_\psi \end{bmatrix} \]
Physical Parameters (from quadrotor_sim.py): mass = 1.0 kg, arm_length = 0.25 m, \(I_{xx} = I_{yy} = 0.0082\;\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2\), \(I_{zz} = 0.0150\;\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2\), \(k_f = 6.8 \times 10^{-6}\), \(k_m = 2.6 \times 10^{-7}\), motor range: 0–838 rad/s.

Section 4: Part 2 — Cascaded Control Architecture in ROS2

The standard control architecture used by virtually all commercial and research flight controllers (DJI, PX4, ArduPilot, BetaFlight) is a cascaded loop structure. Each loop operates at a different frequency, with inner loops running faster than outer loops.

4.1 Three-Loop Cascade

Outer Loop: Position Controller

Rate: 10–50 Hz

Input: Desired position \((x_d, y_d, z_d)\) from mission planner

Output: Desired attitude angles \((\phi_d, \theta_d)\) and thrust \(T_d\)

Sensor: GPS/vision (position), barometer (altitude)

This loop converts position error into desired accelerations, then maps accelerations to desired tilt angles using the small-angle approximation.

Middle Loop: Attitude Controller

Rate: 100–250 Hz

Input: Desired attitude \((\phi_d, \theta_d, \psi_d)\)

Output: Desired angular rates \((p_d, q_d, r_d)\)

Sensor: IMU (accelerometer-derived attitude via complementary or Mahony filter)

Proportional control on attitude error to generate rate setpoints. This decouples attitude from rate dynamics.

Inner Loop: Rate Controller

Rate: 250–1000 Hz

Input: Desired angular rates \((p_d, q_d, r_d)\)

Output: Motor commands (PWM or speed)

Sensor: Gyroscope (direct angular rate measurement, no filtering needed)

Full PID on rate error. This is the tightest loop and must run fastest for stability.

Why Cascaded? A single loop from position to motors would need to invert the full nonlinear dynamics at every step, requiring extremely high bandwidth. By cascading, each loop only needs to stabilize one dynamic mode: the inner rate loop damps oscillations, the attitude loop tracks orientation, and the outer loop tracks position. Each loop sees the next inner loop as an approximately instantaneous actuator (because the inner loop is 5–10x faster).

Section 5: Part 3 — Implementing PID Attitude Controller in ROS2

Below is a complete ROS2 Python node implementing a cascaded PID attitude controller. It subscribes to /imu for current attitude and /attitude_cmd for desired orientation, then publishes torque/thrust commands on /motor_cmd.

5.1 Full PID Attitude Controller Node

#!/usr/bin/env python3 """PID Attitude Controller - ROS2 Node for Quadrotor.""" import rclpy from rclpy.node import Node from sensor_msgs.msg import Imu from geometry_msgs.msg import Vector3 from std_msgs.msg import Float64MultiArray import numpy as np from math import atan2, asin, sqrt class PIDAttitudeController(Node): def __init__(self): super().__init__('pid_attitude_controller') # PID gains: [roll, pitch, yaw] self.kp = np.array([6.0, 6.0, 3.0]) self.ki = np.array([0.5, 0.5, 0.2]) self.kd = np.array([2.0, 2.0, 1.0]) self.integral = np.zeros(3) self.prev_error = np.zeros(3) self.integral_limit = 2.0 self.des_rpy = np.zeros(3) # desired roll, pitch, yaw self.cur_rpy = np.zeros(3) # current roll, pitch, yaw self.cur_rates = np.zeros(3) # current angular rates # Complementary filter state self.alpha = 0.98 self.cf_rpy = np.zeros(3) self.cf_init = False # Subscribers self.create_subscription(Imu, '/imu', self.imu_cb, 10) self.create_subscription(Vector3, '/attitude_cmd', self.cmd_cb, 10) # Publisher self.cmd_pub = self.create_publisher(Float64MultiArray, '/motor_cmd', 10) # Timer at 250 Hz self.dt = 1.0 / 250.0 self.create_timer(self.dt, self.control_loop) self.get_logger().info('PID Attitude Controller started at 250 Hz') def quat_to_euler(self, q): """Convert quaternion (x,y,z,w) to Euler (roll, pitch, yaw).""" x, y, z, w = q.x, q.y, q.z, q.w sinr = 2.0 * (w * x + y * z) cosr = 1.0 - 2.0 * (x * x + y * y) roll = atan2(sinr, cosr) sinp = 2.0 * (w * y - z * x) pitch = asin(max(-1.0, min(1.0, sinp))) siny = 2.0 * (w * z + x * y) cosy = 1.0 - 2.0 * (y * y + z * z) yaw = atan2(siny, cosy) return np.array([roll, pitch, yaw]) def imu_cb(self, msg): """Process IMU: complementary filter for attitude.""" accel_rpy = self.quat_to_euler(msg.orientation) gyro = np.array([msg.angular_velocity.x, msg.angular_velocity.y, msg.angular_velocity.z]) self.cur_rates = gyro if not self.cf_init: self.cf_rpy = accel_rpy self.cf_init = True else: self.cf_rpy = (self.alpha * (self.cf_rpy + gyro * self.dt) + (1 - self.alpha) * accel_rpy) self.cur_rpy = self.cf_rpy def cmd_cb(self, msg): self.des_rpy = np.array([msg.x, msg.y, msg.z]) def control_loop(self): error = self.des_rpy - self.cur_rpy self.integral += error * self.dt self.integral = np.clip(self.integral, -self.integral_limit, self.integral_limit) derivative = -self.cur_rates # use gyro directly torque = (self.kp * error + self.ki * self.integral + self.kd * derivative) msg = Float64MultiArray() msg.data = torque.tolist() self.cmd_pub.publish(msg) def main(): rclpy.init() rclpy.spin(PIDAttitudeController()) rclpy.shutdown() if __name__ == '__main__': main()

5.2 Complementary Filter for Attitude Estimation

The complementary filter fuses gyroscope integration (high-frequency, drift-prone) with accelerometer-derived angles (low-frequency, noisy) to produce a stable attitude estimate:

\[ \hat{\phi}_{k+1} = \alpha\bigl(\hat{\phi}_k + \omega_x \,\Delta t\bigr) + (1 - \alpha)\,\phi_\text{acc} \]

where:

  • \(\alpha \in [0.95, 0.99]\) is the filter coefficient (higher = more trust in gyroscope)
  • \(\omega_x\) is the gyroscope angular rate about the x-axis
  • \(\phi_\text{acc} = \text{atan2}(a_y, a_z)\) is the accelerometer-derived roll angle
  • \(\Delta t\) is the time step between IMU samples
Why not just use the gyroscope? Gyroscope integration drifts over time due to bias. The accelerometer provides an absolute reference (gravity direction) but is noisy and affected by translational acceleration. The complementary filter combines the best of both: short-term accuracy from the gyro and long-term stability from the accelerometer. For production systems, a Mahony or Madgwick filter (quaternion-based) is preferred.

The same principle applies for pitch:

\[ \hat{\theta}_{k+1} = \alpha\bigl(\hat{\theta}_k + \omega_y \,\Delta t\bigr) + (1 - \alpha)\,\theta_\text{acc} \]

where \(\theta_\text{acc} = \text{atan2}\bigl(-a_x,\;\sqrt{a_y^2 + a_z^2}\bigr)\). Yaw cannot be corrected by the accelerometer alone — it requires a magnetometer or visual heading.

Section 6: Part 4 — Applying All 7 Controllers to Quadrotor

In Weeks 1–8 you studied seven control strategies in the abstract. In Week 9 you implemented them as generic ROS2 nodes. Now we map each controller specifically to the quadrotor dynamics and discuss the practical ROS2 implementation considerations.

6.1 Computed Torque Control (CTC)

Concept

Computed Torque Control (also called feedback linearization) cancels the nonlinear terms in the rotational dynamics and replaces them with a desired linear response:

\[ \boldsymbol{\tau} = J\mathbf{u}_d + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times J\boldsymbol{\omega} \]

where \(\mathbf{u}_d = \ddot{\mathbf{q}}_d + K_d(\dot{\mathbf{q}}_d - \dot{\mathbf{q}}) + K_p(\mathbf{q}_d - \mathbf{q})\) is the auxiliary PD input. After cancellation, the closed-loop system reduces to \(\ddot{\mathbf{e}} + K_d\dot{\mathbf{e}} + K_p\mathbf{e} = 0\), which is a linear second-order system with tunable damping.

  • Requires: Accurate inertia matrix \(J\). Sensitive to payload changes.
  • ROS2 Implementation: The Coriolis/gyroscopic term \(\boldsymbol{\omega} \times J\boldsymbol{\omega}\) is computed in each timer callback using the latest gyroscope reading. Publishes torque commands.
  • Best for: Known, well-characterized platforms where the model is accurate.

6.2 Model Predictive Control (MPC)

Concept

Linearize the quadrotor dynamics about the hover operating point. At each control cycle, solve a finite-horizon constrained optimization:

\[ \min_{\mathbf{u}_{0:N-1}} \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} \bigl(\|\mathbf{x}_k - \mathbf{x}_\text{ref}\|_Q^2 + \|\mathbf{u}_k\|_R^2\bigr) + \|\mathbf{x}_N - \mathbf{x}_\text{ref}\|_P^2 \]

subject to \(\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = A\mathbf{x}_k + B\mathbf{u}_k\), thrust limits \(0 \le T \le T_\text{max}\), and angle constraints \(|\phi| \le 30^\circ, |\theta| \le 30^\circ\).

  • Handles: Thrust saturation and angle limits explicitly. Can preview reference trajectories.
  • ROS2 Implementation: Heavier computation; run at 10–20 Hz in a separate thread or as the outer position loop. Use OSQP or acados for the QP solver. Apply only the first control action.
  • Industry: ETH Zurich (aggressive aerobatics), Skydio (obstacle avoidance), DJI Matrice (waypoint tracking).

6.3 Adaptive Control

Concept

Online estimation of uncertain parameters (mass \(m\), inertia \(J\), aerodynamic coefficients) using the regressor form:

\[ \boldsymbol{\tau} = Y(\mathbf{q}, \dot{\mathbf{q}}, \dot{\mathbf{q}}_r, \ddot{\mathbf{q}}_r)\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}} + K_d\mathbf{s} \]

where \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\) is the parameter estimate vector, \(Y\) is the regressor matrix, and \(\mathbf{s} = \dot{\mathbf{e}} + \Lambda\mathbf{e}\) is the sliding variable. The update law is \(\dot{\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}} = -\Gamma^{-1}Y^T\mathbf{s}\).

  • Use case: Quadrotor picking up unknown payloads (mass changes mid-flight), battery depletion changing mass.
  • ROS2 Implementation: Parameter update runs alongside the control law in the same callback. Store \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\) as node state. Publish parameter estimates for monitoring on /param_estimates.

6.4 Robust Control (H-infinity / Sliding Boundary Layer)

Concept

Add a robust compensating term to handle bounded disturbances (wind, ground effect, model uncertainty):

\[ \boldsymbol{\tau} = \boldsymbol{\tau}_\text{nominal} + \boldsymbol{\tau}_\text{robust} \]

where \(\boldsymbol{\tau}_\text{robust} = -\rho\,\text{sgn}(\mathbf{s})\) with \(\rho \geq \|\mathbf{d}\|_\infty\). To avoid chattering, a boundary layer is used: \(\text{sat}(\mathbf{s}/\epsilon)\) replaces \(\text{sgn}(\mathbf{s})\).

  • Use case: Outdoor flight with wind gusts up to 10 m/s, flying near structures (ground effect).
  • ROS2 Implementation: A wind estimator node subscribes to /imu and /odom, estimates disturbance force, and publishes on /wind_estimate. The robust compensator node uses this estimate to set the disturbance bound \(\rho\).

6.5 Backstepping Control

Concept

Backstepping is a natural fit for the cascaded quadrotor structure. Design proceeds in two steps:

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{Step 1 (outer):}\quad &\text{Position error} \;\rightarrow\; \text{Virtual control: desired attitude } (\phi_d, \theta_d, T_d) \\ \text{Step 2 (inner):}\quad &\text{Attitude error} \;\rightarrow\; \text{Actual torques } \boldsymbol{\tau} \end{aligned} \]

Each step uses a Lyapunov function to prove stability. The virtual control from Step 1 becomes the reference for Step 2.

  • Advantage: Formal stability guarantee via Lyapunov analysis. Handles the underactuated nature (4 inputs, 6 DOF) naturally through the virtual control.
  • ROS2 Implementation: Two-node architecture mirrors the two backstepping steps: /backstepping_outer (position) and /backstepping_inner (attitude). The outer node publishes desired attitude for the inner node.

6.6 Sliding Mode Control (SMC)

Concept

Define a sliding surface for each attitude axis:

\[ s_\phi = \dot{e}_\phi + \lambda_\phi\, e_\phi \]

The control law drives the state to the surface and maintains it there:

\[ \tau_\phi = I_{xx}\bigl(\ddot{\phi}_d + \lambda_\phi\,\dot{e}_\phi - \eta\,\text{sat}(s_\phi / \epsilon)\bigr) - (I_{yy} - I_{zz})qr \]
  • Strength: Excellent disturbance rejection for matched disturbances (wind acting through control channels). Invariant to parameter uncertainty once on the surface.
  • Chattering: Mitigated with boundary layer \(\epsilon\) or super-twisting algorithm.
  • ROS2 Implementation: Requires a fast inner loop (500–1000 Hz) to maintain sliding condition. Timer period = 1–2 ms. Use BEST_EFFORT QoS to avoid blocking.

6.7 Nonlinear Control Optimization — iLQR / NMPC (Week 8)

Concept

Rather than linearising the quadrotor around hover once, iLQR (Week 8 §9) linearises around the current-iterate trajectory at every iteration and solves a time-varying LQR in closed form. The discrete-time recursion computes, for \(k = N\!-\!1,\ldots,0\):

\[ k_k = -Q_{uu,k}^{-1} Q_{u,k},\quad K_k = -Q_{uu,k}^{-1} Q_{ux,k} \] \[ u_k^{\text{new}} = \bar u_k + \alpha\,k_k + K_k\,(x_k - \bar x_k) \]

where \(Q_{uu}, Q_{ux}, Q_u\) are Jacobian/Hessian blocks assembled from the nonlinear dynamics \(f\) and cost \(L\) (see Week 8 §9 notation table). Wrapped in a receding-horizon loop this is NMPC.

  • Advantage: Handles the full nonlinear quadrotor model including tilt saturation and aggressive manoeuvres where linearised LQR fails; simultaneously provides an open-loop plan and a local time-varying feedback policy \(K_k\).
  • Limitation: Iterative, higher compute per step than LQR; locally (not globally) optimal; needs warm-starting across samples to meet real-time.
  • ROS2 implementation: Timer callback at 50–100 Hz runs 3–5 iLQR iterations with warm-started control sequence; finite-difference Jacobians for the 6- or 12-state quadrotor; apply \(u_0^\star\), shift the warm-start. See week_09/w11_exercises/task_nmpc_node.py for the unicycle template.
  • Meta-use: iLQR/NMPC is also the offline tuning tool for the other six controllers — minimising a closed-loop cost \(J(\theta)\) over SMC gains \((\lambda,\phi,\eta)\), adaptive \((\Gamma,\sigma)\), robust \((K_p,K_d,\rho,\Lambda)\), or backstepping \((k_1,\ldots,k_n)\) as shown in Week 8 §5–§8.

Quick Quiz: Quadrotor Control in ROS2

Q1. In a cascaded control architecture, why must the inner rate loop run at a higher frequency (e.g., 500 Hz) than the outer position loop (e.g., 50 Hz)?

Answer

The inner loop must be fast enough that it appears as an instantaneous actuator to the outer loop. The rule of thumb is that each inner loop should be 5–10x faster than the loop that commands it. If the rate loop were too slow, the attitude loop would command rate changes that take too long to execute, leading to phase lag and potential instability in the outer loop. Additionally, the angular rate dynamics are the fastest dynamics in the quadrotor system (small inertia, high torques), so the rate controller must sample at or above the Nyquist rate of these dynamics.

Q2. The motor mixing matrix \(M\) has dimensions \(4 \times 4\). Under what condition is \(M\) invertible? What physical situation would make it singular?

Answer

\(M\) is invertible when the four motors can independently produce the four wrench components (thrust, roll torque, pitch torque, yaw torque). It becomes singular if, for example, all motors are collocated (arm length \(d = 0\)), which makes the roll and pitch torque rows identical to zero. It would also be singular if the yaw torque coefficients were all equal (no counter-rotating pairs), as you could not produce differential yaw torque. In the standard plus or X configuration with properly counter-rotating motors, \(M\) is always full rank.

Q3. Why is MPC typically placed in the outer position loop (10–20 Hz) rather than the inner rate loop for a quadrotor?

Answer

MPC involves solving a constrained quadratic program at each cycle, which takes 1–50 ms depending on the horizon length and solver. This computation time is acceptable at 10–20 Hz (50–100 ms period) but would miss deadlines at 500 Hz (2 ms period). The position dynamics are the slowest, so the prediction horizon can be longer without excessive computation. The inner rate loop uses simpler controllers (PID, LQR) that execute in microseconds, ensuring the tight timing needed for angular rate stabilization.

Q4. In the complementary filter \(\hat{\phi}_{k+1} = \alpha(\hat{\phi}_k + \omega_x \Delta t) + (1-\alpha)\phi_\text{acc}\), what happens if \(\alpha = 1.0\)? What if \(\alpha = 0.0\)?

Answer

If \(\alpha = 1.0\), the filter becomes pure gyroscope integration: \(\hat{\phi}_{k+1} = \hat{\phi}_k + \omega_x \Delta t\). This will drift over time due to gyro bias, eventually producing arbitrarily large errors. If \(\alpha = 0.0\), the filter uses only the accelerometer: \(\hat{\phi}_{k+1} = \phi_\text{acc}\). This eliminates drift but produces a very noisy estimate and is invalid during translational acceleration (the accelerometer measures gravity plus linear acceleration, so the gravity-derived angle is wrong during maneuvers). Typical values of \(\alpha = 0.95\)–\(0.99\) balance gyro responsiveness with accelerometer stability.

Q5. Which of the seven controllers would you recommend for a delivery drone that must pick up packages of unknown weight? Justify your choice.

Answer

Adaptive Control is the best choice. When the drone picks up a package, its mass \(m\) and inertia \(J\) change suddenly. Adaptive control uses online parameter estimation to update \(\hat{m}\) and \(\hat{J}\) in real-time, adjusting the control law accordingly. The regressor-based formulation \(\tau = Y(q, \dot{q}, \dot{q}_r, \ddot{q}_r)\hat{\theta}\) can track mass changes within a few seconds. PID would work but would require aggressive integral action and retuning. LQR and MPC would degrade because the linearized model is incorrect for the new mass. Computed Torque would produce incorrect compensation torques until the model is updated. A practical hybrid approach combines Adaptive control with MPC: the adaptive law estimates parameters, and MPC uses the updated model for trajectory optimization.

Summary

Key Takeaways

  • Cascaded Architecture is Standard: The three-loop cascade (position → attitude → rate) is used by every major flight controller (PX4, ArduPilot, DJI, BetaFlight). Each loop runs at a different frequency, with inner loops 5–10x faster than outer loops. In ROS2, this maps naturally to separate nodes communicating via topics.
  • Motor Mixing Matrix is the Bridge: The \(4 \times 4\) mixing matrix \(M\) converts desired thrust and torques into individual motor speed commands. Its inverse \(M^{-1}\) is precomputed and applied at every control cycle. Getting this matrix correct is the first step toward any quadrotor controller.
  • Complementary Filter Enables Attitude Estimation: Fusing gyroscope and accelerometer data with a tunable parameter \(\alpha\) provides a computationally cheap attitude estimate suitable for inner-loop control. Production systems use Mahony or extended Kalman filters.
  • No Single Controller Wins Everything: PID is simple and robust to tune. LQR is energy-optimal near hover. MPC handles constraints and trajectory preview. SMC and Robust control excel at wind rejection. Adaptive control handles changing payloads. Backstepping provides formal stability guarantees. CTC gives exact linearization when the model is accurate.
  • ROS2 Enables Modular Controller Swapping: Because each controller is a separate ROS2 node with standardized topic interfaces (/attitude_cmd in, /motor_cmd out), you can swap controllers without modifying the rest of the system. This is the power of the ROS2 architecture for rapid prototyping and comparison.
  • Real-Time Matters: The inner rate loop must execute within 1–2 ms. PID and LQR easily meet this. MPC requires careful solver selection and runs in the outer loop. Use timer-based callbacks, pre-allocate memory, and avoid heap allocation in control loops.

Appendix: Review Material — ROS2 Control Implementation

Question 1 (25 marks)

Explain the architecture of a ROS2 control node for a robotic system. In your answer:

  1. Identify the topics a typical ROS2 control node subscribes to (sensor feedback) and publishes (actuator commands). (5 marks)
  2. Explain how the control loop rate is managed using create_timer() and why deterministic timing is important for controller stability. (8 marks)
  3. Compare timer-based control loops (fixed-rate callbacks) versus event-driven control loops (triggered on new sensor data). Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of each approach in terms of latency, jitter, and computational load. (12 marks)

Model Answer — Q1

Part (a): A typical ROS2 control node subscribes to state-feedback topics such as /odom (nav_msgs/Odometry) for position and velocity, /imu (sensor_msgs/Imu) for angular velocity and linear acceleration, and /joint_states (sensor_msgs/JointState) for joint-level feedback. It publishes actuator commands on topics like /cmd_vel (geometry_msgs/Twist), /motor_cmd (std_msgs/Float64MultiArray), or /joint_commands.

Part (b): The control loop rate is managed by calling self.create_timer(period_sec, callback) in the node constructor. For example, self.create_timer(0.01, self.control_callback) creates a 100 Hz control loop. Deterministic timing is critical because the discrete-time controller gains (Kp, Ki, Kd) are tuned for a specific sample period \(\Delta t\). Jitter in the control period effectively changes the derivative and integral computations, potentially causing instability. The integral term accumulates error multiplied by \(\Delta t\): if \(\Delta t\) varies unpredictably, the integral grows incorrectly. For derivative action, \( u_d = K_d \cdot (e_k - e_{k-1})/\Delta t \), inconsistent \(\Delta t\) amplifies noise.

Part (c):

AspectTimer-Based (Fixed-Rate)Event-Driven (Sensor-Triggered)
TimingFixed period (e.g., 100 Hz), decoupled from sensor rateFires when new sensor message arrives
LatencyAverage latency = half the control period; worst case = full periodMinimal latency — processes data immediately upon arrival
JitterLow jitter if OS scheduling is adequate; consistent \(\Delta t\)Jitter depends on sensor timing; inconsistent \(\Delta t\)
Computational loadPredictable, evenly spaced CPU usageBursty — may overload if multiple sensors arrive simultaneously
Best forInner-loop controllers needing guaranteed rate (PID, LQR)High-level planners, vision-based control where data is sparse

In practice, most flight controllers (PX4, ArduPilot) use timer-based loops for attitude and rate control, with event-driven callbacks only for updating the latest sensor data into shared buffers that the timer callback reads.

Question 2 (25 marks)

You implemented PID, LQR, and MPC as separate ROS2 control nodes in Week 9. Compare their computational requirements per control cycle by answering the following:

  1. List the main mathematical operations each controller performs per cycle (e.g., matrix multiply, quadratic program solve). (9 marks)
  2. Estimate the approximate floating-point operation (FLOP) count per cycle for a system with \(n = 12\) states and \(m = 4\) inputs. (8 marks)
  3. Which of the three controllers requires hard real-time guarantees and why? What ROS2 mechanisms (executor type, QoS settings) help achieve this? (8 marks)

Model Answer — Q2

Part (a):

  • PID: Per axis: 1 subtraction (error), 1 multiply + 1 add (P term), 1 multiply + 1 add (I accumulation), 1 subtraction + 1 division (D term), 1 addition (sum). For 4 axes: ~40 FLOPs total. Constant time O(1).
  • LQR: One matrix-vector multiplication \( \mathbf{u} = -K(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_d) \) where \(K \in \mathbb{R}^{4 \times 12}\). This is \(4 \times 12 = 48\) multiplies and \(4 \times 11 = 44\) additions, plus 12 subtractions for the error vector. Total: ~104 FLOPs. Also O(1) since K is precomputed.
  • MPC: At each cycle, solves a constrained quadratic program (QP). For a horizon \(N = 20\) with 12 states and 4 inputs, the QP has \(20 \times 4 = 80\) decision variables and \(20 \times (12 + 4 \times 2) = 400\) constraints. An interior-point solver typically requires 10–30 iterations, each involving a \(80 \times 80\) matrix factorization. Approximate cost: \(O(N^3 m^3)\) per solve, roughly \(10^4\)–\(10^6\) FLOPs.

Part (b):

ControllerFLOPs / CycleTypical Time on ARM Cortex
PID~40< 1 μs
LQR~104~2 μs
MPC (N=20)~1051–50 ms

Part (c): PID and LQR both require hard real-time guarantees because they run in the inner attitude/rate loop at 250–1000 Hz. Missing a deadline causes immediate instability in an airborne vehicle. MPC typically runs at 10–50 Hz in the outer position loop and can tolerate slight deadline misses (the previous solution is applied if the new one is late). In ROS2, real-time is supported by: (1) using a SingleThreadedExecutor on a dedicated thread with SCHED_FIFO priority, (2) setting QoS to BEST_EFFORT reliability with small queue depths to avoid blocking, (3) pre-allocating all memory to avoid heap allocation in the control loop, and (4) using the rclcpp real-time-safe utilities or the realtime_tools package.
